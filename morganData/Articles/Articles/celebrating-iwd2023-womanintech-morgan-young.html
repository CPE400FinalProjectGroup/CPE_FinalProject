Celebrating Women in Technology for #IWD2023

This year, the theme of International Women's Day is "DigitALL: Innovation and technology for gender equality." So I thought it was only fitting to celebrate #womenintech today and provide a little insight on the current state of women in tech, share a little bit of history, and show off my bright outlook for the future.

Women in Tech, the Numbers at-a-glance

  • 28% of overall tech industry workforce is women (2022), according to Zippia
  • 26% of computing jobs are held by women (2022), according to Built In
  • 20% of undergraduate Computer Science degrees were granted to women (2022), according to UN Report
  • 1.9% of available venture capital funding went to women-led startups in 2022, according to Tech Crunch

The Big Picture: Past Pioneers

The first row of the cover photo highlights past pioneers of computing and technology. Going from left to right, the first photo features Ada Lovelace, an English countess, who became the first person to publish an algorithm. She was also well-known for her notes on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine, which was quite difficult to explain or even grasp. The second photo features Margaret Hamilton, who served as the Director of Software Engineering for the Apollo Project and later started two tech companies, High Order Software and Hamilton Technologies. The third photo features Grace Hopper, who is regarded as one of the most significant pioneers of computing for her contributions to Mark I, the first programmable computer, UNIVAC I, the first known large-scale electronic computer on the market in 1950, and the A compiler, the first compiler to process an English-based programming language. She is also the namesake of the world's largest conference for women in computing, GHC or the Grace Hopper Conference. The fourth and fifth photos depict Katherine G. Johnson and Dorothy Vaughan, two of the three titular characters in the popular movie, Hidden Figures. Katherine G. Johnson calculated the trajectory for Alan Shepherd's space flight, the launch window for the 1961 Mercury mission, the trajectory for the 1969 Apollo 11 flight, and many more. She was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2015, for her influential work in space and computing. Dorothy Vaughan worked as a supervisor for NACA (the predecessor to NASA), and was well-known for her expertise in Fortran and other programming languages. The sixth photo in the top row is Hedy Lamarr, who was better known as a Hollywood starlet, but also made significant contributions in electronics engineering. She along with George Antheil, created and attained a patent for a frequency-hopping system, intended for Navy radio-controlled torpedoes. Although the technology was never adopted, the principles of their work was incorporated into GPS and Bluetooth technology. The seventh photo shows Annie Easley, another innovator at NASA. She was known for her development and implementation of code that analyzed alternative power technology, and her contributions to the Centaur rocket stage. The last photo in the first row is Adele Goldberg, most famous for her role as a co-developer of Smalltalk, one of the first Object-Oriented Programming languages (OOP).

Be a Team Player!

Here are a few ways you can help break the glass ceiling for women in tech even if you yourself are not a woman in tech:

  • Consider volunteering, supporting, or getting involved in any of the organizations mentioned above
  • Offer mentorship to aspiring women in tech, if you're in a position to do so
  • Be proactive in outreach/inclusion programs at work like ERGs and BERGs (Employee Resource Groups & Business Employee Resource Groups)
  • If you're in management, educate yourself and be proactive in cultivating an inclusive, and empowering workplace for women
