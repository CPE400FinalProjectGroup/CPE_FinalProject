Landing a Fortune 100 Internship as a Freshman: 5 Key Takeaways


1Ô∏è‚É£ When you're starting out your career, your resume is what will get you interviews.

I've seen a thousand different strategies to land an internship when you're a freshman or sophomore. But ultimately, when you're that early in your career, it comes down to your resume. How else are you going to stand out in a stack of papers, especially if you're cold applying? There are two key actions from this insight: resume writing and resume building. Firstly, you have to spend time building the contents of your resume. If you feel like you don't have a lot to put on your resume, start working on things you can add i.e. do a project, compete in a competition, or take on a part-time job. Once you feel like you've beefed up the contents of your resume, work on writing your resume in a way that tells a compelling story about you. How do you do this? Use specific details, depth, and of course, quantifying metrics.

2Ô∏è‚É£ The more you interview, the better you'll get at it.

Interviewing is a skill that can be improved, but frequency and consistency are key. I went through 19 interviews by the end of my freshman internship hunt. Luckily, Disney was my last one. By that point, I was pretty dang good at interviewing, but it took me 18 interviews to get there. Interviewing is your chance to leave a lasting impression on your recruiter or hiring manager and you don't have a lot of time to do it. So remember these things ~ know your resume inside and out since that's what they're interviewing you on, speak with clarity and confidence (this shows strong communication skills), and lastly, people want to work with people that they like. Sometimes an interview is as simple as getting someone to like you within 5-20 minutes.

3Ô∏è‚É£ It's a numbers game. Treat it like one.

Landing an internship this early in your undergraduate career is an uphill battle to put it lightly. It's really all about finding 1 or 2 people that are willing to "take a chance on you" (as much as I hate saying that). This is because when you're a freshman or sophomore, you don't have a lot of what I call "social proof" or "social-professional capital." You probably haven't held that many jobs yet, and you definitely haven't finished school yet. That's okay though! When you do find those people that are willing to bet on you, that's how you identify good leaders and mentors. That's how I found my first few mentors ü•∞. So apply to as many internships as you humanly can. I applied to 150 as a freshman and I landed 10 first-round interviews and 3 offers from that initial 150. One of my mentors called it, "increasing my chances."

4Ô∏è‚É£ Don't take it personally, a rejection is NOT a reflection of you.

150 applications - 3 offers = 147 rejections. I was rejected 147 times. Also, that's a 2% success rate, which, when you think about it, sucks. But that's the difference between math and job hunting. You only have to get it right once. And rejection is NOT a reflection of you, your qualifications, your talent, your intellect, or your character. It just means that it wasn't the right opportunity for you. Plus, as I've mentioned, applying for internships as an underclassman is ROUGH. Companies are generally less inclined to hire freshmen and sophomores for HR reasons and various other trivial reasons. So getting rejected from a certain job could literally just mean that they have a policy against hiring underclassmen. Remember that.


5Ô∏è‚É£ It's not about being the smartest or the best.

Yes, I just said that. I guarantee you, out of the applicant pools I was in for the 3 offers I received, I was not the smartest. I DEFINITELY wasn't the best technically. I bombed my first AND second coding interviews. I get average scores in all of my CS classes. Did that matter when it came down to it? No. The people that made me offers liked my drive, my work ethic, my seemingly boundless energy, and my communication skills. It was all of the intangible stuff. At that point, when you're in the interview process, you can be confident that you and other candidates are probably on the same playing field as far as qualifications go. So what makes you stand out? Getting the interviewer to remember you, leaving a lasting impression on them, and not being afraid to stand out.
